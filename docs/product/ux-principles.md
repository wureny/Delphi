These principles guide the user experience design of Delphi.

1. Expose thesis state first
   The primary view should show the user's theses, conviction levels, freshness and open questions. A blank chat prompt does not help maintain context.
2. Highlight counter-evidence
   Contradictory information must be surfaced rather than hidden. Summary cards should visually differentiate supporting and contradicting evidence.
3. Optimise for "what changed?"
   Users should quickly see what has materially changed since their last review. Provide clear diff-style summaries that map new information to assumptions.
4. Ground every claim
   Every AI-generated assertion that influences a thesis should display its source. If evidence is thin or uncertain, label it clearly.
5. Design for correctability
   Classifications must be editable. Provide intuitive affordances for the user to correct mistakes and record the adjustment.
6. Reduce cognitive load
   Break complex information into digestible sections. Use progressive disclosure and avoid long unstructured paragraphs.
7. Preserve uncertainty and nuance
   Not all questions have clear answers. The UI should support ambiguous or incomplete data.
8. Avoid jargon
   Do not expose ontology or graph concepts to users. Use human-readable language such as thesis, evidence, assumption, risk, catalyst, decision and decision trace.
9. Handle all states
   Design explicit empty, loading, error, partial and stale data states.
10. Make decisions auditable
   Users should be able to revisit why they made a decision, what evidence supported it and how conviction evolved.
