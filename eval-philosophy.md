Delphi integrates AI to classify evidence, detect counter‑evidence, summarise changes and assist with decision traces.
To ensure reliable behaviour, we employ a rigorous evaluation framework.  These principles guide how we design, implement and review evals.

Behaviour‑first evaluation

Evals should reflect externally observable behaviour rather than implementation specifics.  They answer: does the system do what the user expects?
For instance, an eval might check that new evidence is correctly classified as supporting or contradicting a thesis and that a source citation is present.  It should not test internal model prompts or hidden chain‑of‑thought.

Comprehensive coverage

Evals must cover:

* Positive cases – where the AI behaves correctly on straightforward examples.
* Negative cases – where the AI should not misclassify unrelated or irrelevant information.
* Ambiguous cases – where evidence is neutral or unclear; the AI should mark uncertainty rather than fabricate confidence.
* Adversarial cases – where the input might tempt the model to hallucinate or give ungrounded advice.  Evals should enforce refusal or caution.
* Stale or irrelevant source cases – ensure the system does not falsely tie outdated information to current theses.

Grounding and citations

Any investment‑relevant statement generated by the system must cite its source(s).  Evals should check for the presence and integrity of citations.
When evidence is insufficient, the model should explicitly acknowledge uncertainty.  Evals should penalise answers that confidently assert unsupported claims.

No financial advice leakage

Delphi must not produce buy/sell recommendations or price targets unless explicitly framed by the user.  Evals should include prompts that might elicit such advice (e.g., “Should I buy this stock?”) and ensure the system responds by clarifying its limits.

Decision trace integrity

Decision trace generation must produce a human‑auditable ledger: timestamp, actor, prior thesis state, new evidence, changed assumptions, decision, rationale, sources and follow‑up actions.
Evals should ensure that decision traces do not expose the model’s chain‑of‑thought and that all fields are present and correctly populated.

Continuous improvement

Evals are not “one and done.”  As new behaviours and models are introduced, the evaluation suite must evolve.  Evals should live alongside code; no AI‑powered feature is complete without passing evals.
When regressions are found, new evals must be added to prevent recurrence.
